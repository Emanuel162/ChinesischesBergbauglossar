Chronological list of sources for the history of mining terminology in premodern southwest China
Title Author Short Reference Region Period Description
Song Yinxing Song Yingxing 宋應星 [chapter „Yin 銀] in Tiangong kaiwu 天工開物. In DNKCTL j.1, 25a-27a. undefined, partly Chuxiong in Yunnan Roughly 1620s, partly earlier Song generally describes technology without providing geographic specification. The chapter on silver specifically refers to Chuxiong in Yunnan for a specific technology. Song travelled to Yunnan and presumably obtained much of his information on silver metallurgy here. The genesis of the text may be roughly dated to the 1620s to early 1630s. Some sources may be older. Song may have combined older ters with current terms from different regions, probably mainly from his native Jiangxi and adjoining regions and Yunnan. The annotated edition in DNKCTL adds annotations that explain Song’s terminology by current Yunnanese terms.
Gejiu Anonymous section on Gejiu in the chapter “Changwu” 廠務 (Mining matters) in Mengzi xianzhi 蒙自縣志 (Kangxi 康熙 ed.), j.2, 9b-13a. Identical in Gujin tushu jicheng 古今圖書集成: Zhifang dian 職方典, j 1474, p. 41-43 Gejiu, southern Yunnan Roughly 1700, perhaps about 1600-1640

The gazetteer was completed in 1710, while the compilation of Gujin tushu jicheng lasted from 1700 to 1720. The text therefore dates no later than 1710. Clearly written by a member of the local administration as a description of the mining area, the text is unusually long and detailed. It may have been produced in the context of the gazetteer preparation or already held in the local government as a aid in monitoring the mines.

Two details might indicate that it was older:

First, the text specifies that Gejiu is a xiang 鄉 subcounty and that the inhabitants of the main village Gejiu or the entire area were registered in the baojia system. Because Qing gazetteers record no xiang of this name and the baojia system was not much emphasized in the Qing administration of Yunnan, this might date the text to the late Ming.

Second, the description on mining activities in the text diverges from the following taxation records. This might indicate a time gap between the text and the taxation records, which can be safely dated to the late Kangxi period. I have not analysed this divergence in detail.
Chen Nieheng Chen Nieheng 陳聂恒 Ten entries in juan 9 in Bianzhou jianwenlu 邊州聞見錄 (Things heard and seen in the borderland districts). Manuscript dated 1720. Juan 9, no pagination [6a-12a]. Digitized in Zhongguo fangzhiku. Yiliang, northeast Yunnan ca. 1705-1714

Chen Nieheng’s biji collection is a private work of notes recorded during official appointments to the border regions of southwestern Sichuan. It contains ten entries on copper mining in Yiliang. The region was part of Sichuan until 1726, when it was added to Yunnan province. Direct Qing rule was enforced following several military campaigns against the Yi rulers by 1730.

The relevant sections are dated by Chen’s stay in Yiliang in 1714 and mentions of events in the mines that occurred during the first decade of the century. Chen records personal observations, information obtained on site and also draws on materials held in the government offices. As a private record not modelled on precedents, the recorded information is highly reliable.
Wang Taiyue Wang Taiyue 王太岳 “Tongshan yin yibaiershi yun” 銅山吟一百二十韻 (120 rhymes on the copper mountains). In Zhang Yingchang 張應昌, ed. Qingshi duo 清詩鐸, juan 24, 6a-8b. Yunnan Ca 1770s Wang Taiyue served as provincial treasurer of Yunnan in the later 1770s and in this capacity was responsible for copper exploitation. Upon leaving the province, he left a lengthy proposal for reforms in the copper procurement system. This text strongly emphasized economic hardships inflicted upon the commoner population. Due to this focus, it contains no technical terms. The poem, which contains three technical terms, presumably dates to his tenure in Yunnan.
Quanshu Anonymous “Tongzheng quanshu zixun gechang dui” 銅政全書咨詢各厰對 (Answers to inquiries by several mine [officials]) is an excerpt from Wang Chang’s 王昶 Tongzheng quanshu 銅政全書. The excerpt was included in DNKCTL j.1, 29a-32b and thus survived, while the handbook of the Yunnan copper administration was lost. Yunnan 1778-1780 The text presumably a fact-finding inquiry undertaken by the provincial government. It consists of seven questions and answers by six local officials who were responsible for copper mines in their jurisdictions. For unknown reasons, the answers record conditions in middling and lesser mines only. Questions on organizational, economic and technical issues with answers provided by six local officials, rich in technical terms. By records on some of the participating local officials, the inquiry can be dated to about 1778-1780.
Sun+Fukang’an Sun Shiyi 孫士毅, Fukang’an 福康安 “Chen Diantong shiyi shu” 陳滇銅事宜疏 (Memorial on Yunnan copper matters), 1777;
“Fuzou Diansheng tongqian gechang wuyong fengbi shu” 覆奏滇省銅鉛各厰毋庸封閉疏 (Memorial repeating that closing the copper and lead-zinc mines in Yunnan is unnecessary). In Qinchuan jushi 琴川居士, comp. HuangQing zouyi 皇清奏議 (Memorial of the Qing dynasty), juan 62, 6b-10a and juan 68, 21a-22b. c.1796–1820. Repr. In XX SKQS, vol. 473. 		Yunnan 1777, 1795 Two memorials by governors of Yunnan province on problems in copper mining represent the only two examples of memorials in the collection that contain technical terms.
Tan Cui Tan Cui 檀萃 The sections on metal mines in the Chapter “Zhi jinshi” 志金石 (Minerals) in Tan Cui 檀萃, Dianhai yuhengzhi 滇海虞衡志, juan 2. Orig. 1799. In Wenyinglou yudi congshu 問影樓輿地叢書. [Beijing]: 新昌胡氏, 1908. Digitized in Zhongguo fangzhiku. Yunnan ca. 1750s-1780s

Tan Cui was appointed magistrate of Luquan, the county adjacent to the main copper mining region in Dongchuan prefecture in the mid 1770s. After his dismissal in disgrace, he stayed in the province until about 1800 as private scholar. Numerous quotes reflect that he wall highly regarded. He presumably lived in Kunming and composed Dianhai yuhengzhi 滇海虞衡志 during this period. The work is a private gazetteer. Shi Fan 師範 copyedited and published the work in 1799.

The section on copper extraction, a paragraph on silver, and a summary on zinc, iron and tin contain many technical terms. The section on copper indicates two sources, Nongbu suoji 農部鎖錄, the Luquan gazetteer that Tan Cui himself compiled, presumably in the mid-1780s, an a work identified as “Venerable Zhang’s scattered notes” 張君《雜記》. This otherwise unknown work appears to be by an author who lived in in Kunming during the mid-18th century.
Wu Zhenyu Wu Zhenyu 吳振棫 “Changshu sishou” 厰述四首 (Four poems telling of the mines). In ibid, Huayiguan shicao 花宜館詩鈔 (Poetry by Wu Zhenyu), juan 3, 12ab. Orig. 1665. Reprint in XXSKQS, vol. 1521. Yunnan Ca. 1823, terminology 1750s-1780s

The long poem with annotations by the author contains numerous technical terms. The terminology is fully congruent with Tan Cui, indicating that this work was the source of Wu’s knowledge.

In Wu Zheyu’s chronologically arranged poetry collection, “Changshu” appears between poems on the Dali region. This dates the work in his tenure in this prefecture, which is roughly datable between 1822 and 1824.

Ni Shenshu Ni Shenshu 倪慎樞 “Caitong liantong ji” 采銅煉銅記 (Copper extraction and smelting). In DNKCTL, juan 1, 33a-37b. Yunnan Roughly 1830 The author appears in the records as scholar and a native of Kunming, mentioned in the 1830s and 1840s. Nothing on the context of the descriptive account of copper extraction is known. The text is rich in technical terms.
Wang Song Wang Song 王崧 “Kuangchang cailian pian” 礦廠採煉篇 (Extraction and smelting in the mines ). In DNKCTL, juan 1, 28a-32b. Yunnan Ca. 1830 Wang Song 王崧, a scholar hailing from the Dali region, evidently wrote “Kuangchang cailian pian” 礦廠採煉篇 for the provincial gazetteer. Wang headed the editing team from the outset of the project in 1826, probably into the early 1830s. He left over to differences on the scholarly standards of compilation. Wang Song’s article is rich in technical terms. It is a composite text that provides no sources but reassembles roughly a quarter of the two texts quoted or excerpted in Tan Cui. The remainder of the text may draw on further materials as well as his own observations and knowledge. This text thus represents records from about 1760 to 1830.
Wang Song [Xu Jinsheng徐金生] Diankuang tulue 滇礦圖略 (Briell illustrated account of the Yunnan mines). In DNKCTL, juan 1, 1a-24b. Probably Dongchuan 1830s

The text is a topical descriptive glossary and contains over 200 terms. It covers mining and smelting technologies as well as administration and control and some aspects of socio-economic and religious structures within mining companies and communities.

Together with the illustrations in this work, I attribute this section to Xu Jinsheng 徐金生 (?-c. 1840), a local official who served in three localities Yunnan for over a decade. He left his final appointment as Dongchuan prefect in 1839 because of the death of a parent.

The text can be dated to the later 1830s. If Xu was the author, he presumably drew on materials held in the Dongchuan prefectural offices, while possibly also using records from his previous tenures. The initial booklet may have been a local Dongchuan handbook or a work put together as a pocket-handbook by Xu for the convenience of his peers in Yunnan. It may have been a cumulative work that mainly reflects conditions in Dongchuan, or – less probably - a commissioned work that reflects Dongchuan specifically in the later 1830s.
Huang Mengju Huang Mengju 黄梦菊 “Bing changqing tiaokuan” 禀厰情條款 (Itemized report on the situation in the mines).” In Diannan shishi 滇南事實 (Factual titbits from Yunnan), 57b-66a. Orig imprint by Yudetang 裕德堂, 1849. Dongchuan ca. 1845 Report on silver and zinc mines in Huize; rich in technical terms
Song Gengping Song Gengping 宋賡平 Kuangxue xinyao xinbian礦學心要新編 (Essentials of Mining, New Edition). Chengdu: Guangshi shanfang, 1902. Western Sichuan Around 1900 Textbook for a mining school; collection of observations during survey of mines, rich in technical terms.
